The Impeachment Proceedings Against Philippine Vice President Sara Duterte

Comprehensive Analysis & Timeline MANILA — April 23, 2026
The impeachment proceedings against Vice President Sara Duterte-Carpio represent one of the most consequential constitutional confrontations in recent Philippine history. As the House Committee on Justice enters the final stages of probable cause determination, the case encompasses allegations of fund misuse, unexplained wealth, and threats against top officials — with profound implications for the 2028 presidential election.

I. Background: The Dissolution of the UniTeam Alliance

Sara Duterte-Carpio and President Ferdinand R. Marcos Jr. were elected in a landslide victory in 2022 under the “UniTeam” banner, an alliance that united two of the Philippines’ most powerful political dynasties. The partnership, however, frayed almost immediately amid tensions over policy direction and the distribution of political power.

The relationship deteriorated into open hostility by late 2024. A pivotal moment occurred on November 23, 2024, when Duterte-Carpio stated during an online press conference that she had instructed someone to kill President Marcos, First Lady Liza Araneta-Marcos, and then-House Speaker Martin Romualdez should any harm befall her. “If I die, don’t stop until you have killed them,” she said, adding, “No joke, no joke.” The presidential palace characterized the remarks as an “active threat.” Duterte-Carpio later maintained that her comments were misinterpreted.

Simultaneously, investigations into the Vice President’s use of confidential funds surfaced. Congressional inquiries revealed that over ₱612.5 million in confidential and intelligence funds allocated to the Office of the Vice President (OVP) and the Department of Education (DepEd)—which Duterte-Carpio headed as Secretary from 2022 to 2024—had been expended under questionable circumstances.

II. The 2025 Impeachment and Supreme Court Intervention

The first impeachment complaints were filed in December 2024. A fifth complaint, endorsed by 215 members of the House of Representatives, was transmitted to the Senate on February 5, 2025. The articles accused her of betrayal of public trust, graft, bribery, culpable violation of the Constitution, and the alleged assassination plot.

Before the Senate could convene, Duterte-Carpio’s legal team filed a petition with the Supreme Court arguing that earlier committee-level complaints triggered the constitutional prohibition against initiating more than one impeachment proceeding against the same official within a single year.

July 25, 2025 — Supreme Court ruling (13-0): The Court held that the impeachment complaint was barred by the one-year rule under Article XI, Section 3(5) of the Constitution. It clarified that it was “not absolving Vice President Duterte from any of the charges against her, but any subsequent impeachment complaint may only be filed starting Feb. 6, 2026.”

The ruling drew mixed reactions: Duterte-Carpio’s defense welcomed the upholding of “the rule of law,” while opposition groups called it a “stain” on the Court’s reputation.

III. The 2026 Impeachment Proceedings

True to the Supreme Court timeline, new impeachment complaints were filed beginning February 2, 2026. Two principal complaints survived screening: the “Saballa et al.” complaint (endorsed by Rep. Leila de Lima) and the “Cabrera complaint” (filed by lawyer Nathaniel Cabrera, endorsed by Deputy Speaker Ortega and Rep. Abante).

The House Committee on Justice, chaired by Rep. Gerville Luistro, undertook a methodical evaluation:

  • FEB 23 Complaints formally referred.
  • MAR 2 Committee voted 22-10 to set aside the first complaint as premature; declared third and fourth complaints sufficient in form.
  • MAR 4 Both complaints ruled sufficient in substance.
  • MAR 16 Duterte-Carpio submitted a 15-page verified answer ad cautelam.
  • MAR 18 Hearings scheduled: March 25, April 14, April 22, and April 29.

The Vice President has repeatedly challenged the proceedings before the Supreme Court. On April 8, 2026, the Court declined to issue a temporary restraining order, instead directing respondents to file comments.

IV. Key Allegations

The impeachment complaints rest on several interrelated accusations, summarized in the table below.

A. Misuse of Confidential Funds

At the core is the alleged misuse of ₱612.5 million. The Commission on Audit (COA) affirmed the disallowance of ₱73.28 million in OVP confidential fund spending for December 2022. Forensic evidence from the NBI revealed that handwritten entries on 36 acknowledgment receipts were written by as few as seven individuals. The PSA verified that out of 677 names submitted for verification, 405 had no record of birth and 398 had no record in PSA databases — including the now-infamous “Mary Grace Piattos.”

B. Unexplained Wealth and SALN Discrepancies

During the April 22 hearing, the Anti-Money Laundering Council (AMLC) presented a summary showing approximately ₱4.425 billion in deposits and ₱1.5 billion in withdrawals across accounts linked to the Vice President and her husband, lawyer Manases Carpio, from 2006 to 2025. The AMLC identified 313 covered transactions and 17 suspicious transactions in Duterte-Carpio’s accounts. The disparity between declared net worth (₱88.5 million) and recorded inflows (₱4.4 billion) was described by lawmakers as a potential “smoking gun.”

C. The Alleged Assassination Threat

The November 23, 2024 statements remain a central element. Duterte-Carpio has denied issuing a threat, insisting that “they are the only ones saying there’s an assassination … I did not say that.” The complaints characterize the remarks as destabilizing and unbecoming of a high official.

D. Bribery Allegations

Former high-ranking DepEd officials testified that they received cash envelopes of ₱50,000 each on multiple occasions during Duterte-Carpio’s tenure as Secretary. She has dismissed these claims, arguing that complainants failed to present “competent evidence” of unlawful use of public funds.

E. Witness Testimony & Protection

Ramil Madriaga, a self-confessed former aide, testified that he personally delivered ₱125 million in confidential funds over an 11-day period. The Committee has granted a petition for adequate protection for Madriaga, which may include transfer of custody.

V. Recent Developments: The “Forensic Truth” Phase

The April 22, 2026 hearing marked what Committee Chair Luistro termed the “forensic truth” phase — a transition from witness testimony to a document-and-data confrontation over alleged unexplained wealth. Lawmakers examined SALNs, SEC records, tax returns, and AMLC reports.

  • AMLC Executive Director Ronel Buenaventura confirmed that 18 out of 18 randomly selected transactions from former Senator Trillanes’ affidavit appeared in agency records.
  • The AMLC disclosed that Duterte-Carpio was the “subject of suspicion” in 27 confidential reports filed between August 2024 and January 2026, citing suspected drug trafficking, graft, and malversation.
  • The Committee examined nearly ₱6.8 billion in total flagged transactions across the couple’s accounts.

Vice President Duterte-Carpio was absent from the April 22 hearing — her third consecutive non-appearance. Chair Luistro noted that “the chair right in front of us, it remains empty,” and emphasized that proceedings would continue based on documents and sworn testimonies.

The Committee has scheduled its fourth and final hearing for April 29, 2026. A committee report may be transmitted to the plenary when Congress resumes on May 4.

VI. Political Implications and Defense Strategy

The impeachment unfolds against the backdrop of Duterte-Carpio’s declared candidacy for the 2028 presidential election. A Senate conviction would result in permanent disqualification from public office.

Political analysts note that the May 2025 mid-term elections — where Duterte-aligned candidates won five of twelve open Senate seats — have altered the calculus. University of the Philippines professor Jean Franco observed that “the political context will be very different, especially now that Sara declared her candidacy.”

President Marcos has publicly stated he opposes the impeachment while asserting he is powerless to intervene. Palace Press Officer Claire Castro reiterated in February 2026 that Malacañang “had no role in the filing of the complaints and would respect the constitutional process.”

Duterte-Carpio’s defense strategy rests on arguments that complaints lack “ultimate facts,” that COA disallowances remain under appeal, and that the Committee applies double standards. Her answer ad cautelam calls the allegations “false, misleading, and mere conclusions of fact and law.”

VII. Conclusion and Outlook

The impeachment proceedings stand at a critical juncture. The House Committee on Justice is expected to conclude its probable cause determination by April 29, 2026. Should the House endorse the articles by the required one-third vote, the case proceeds to the Senate for trial — where a two-thirds majority is needed for conviction.

The proceedings test the resilience of Philippine constitutional mechanisms in navigating politically charged accountability processes. Regardless of outcome, the case will leave an indelible mark on Philippine political jurisprudence and will shape the contours of the 2028 presidential contest.
