The Blinded Sentinel: Samuel Martires and the Systematic Erosion of Accountability

The Blinded Sentinel

Samuel Martires and the Systematic Erosion of Accountability in the Philippine Ombudsman

In a democracy strained by patronage and power imbalances, the Office of the Ombudsman stands as the constitutional bulwark against corruption — an independent guardian tasked with investigating graft, protecting public funds, and upholding ethical governance.

Yet during Samuel Reyes Martires’ tenure from 2018 to 2025, this institution appeared to undergo a profound transformation: from aggressive watchdog to a selective shield that critics argue insulated the powerful while dimming the lights on transparency.

Samuel Reyes Martires served as Ombudsman of the Philippines from July 2018 until his retirement on July 27, 2025. He was succeeded by Justice Secretary Jesus Crispin “Boying” Remulla.

The Davao Roots: A Foundational Favor?

The origins of Martires’ perceived alignment with the Duterte family trace back more than a decade before his appointment as Ombudsman. In 2011, while serving as a Sandiganbayan justice, Martires authored the decision dismissing graft charges against then-Davao City Mayor Rodrigo Duterte and several officials. The case stemmed from procedural violations in a government demolition project.

This ruling set a precedent. When Duterte ascended to the presidency in 2016, Martires’ career accelerated.

The Ascent to Power

Elevated to the Supreme Court, Martires became one of the key votes in the controversial 2018 quo warranto petition that ousted Chief Justice Maria Lourdes Sereno. The unprecedented maneuver bypassed the constitutional impeachment process.

Sereno herself sought Martires’ inhibition, citing bias, but he participated in the 8-6 majority decision that removed her.

Policy Shifts: Closing the Windows of Scrutiny

1. Restricting SALN Access

The Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth (SALN) has long been a cornerstone of transparency under Republic Act 6713. In 2020, Martires issued Memorandum Circular No. 1 that severely restricted public and media access to SALNs filed with the Office of the Ombudsman.

Requests now required authorization from the officials themselves, effectively turning a public document into a near-private record. Martires defended the move by claiming SALNs had been “weaponized” against government officials.

In October 2025, successor Ombudsman Remulla reversed this policy, restoring broader public access.

2. Abolishing Lifestyle Checks

In September 2020, Martires announced the end of routine lifestyle checks on public officials. His rationale: “Wealth is not proof of corruption.”

While accurate in isolation, this policy removed a critical investigative tool that had previously linked lavish lifestyles to unexplained assets. Without lifestyle audits cross-referenced against SALNs, proving illicit enrichment became exponentially harder.

3. Muffling the Commission on Audit

Martires also urged keeping preliminary findings of the Commission on Audit (COA) private until final resolution, arguing that early observations created unfair “innuendos.”

“These changes were not isolated; they formed a coherent architecture that prioritized procedural protections for the accused over proactive detection.”

Quiet Victories for the Powerful

In 2019, Martires quietly granted a motion for reconsideration in an administrative case against Senator Joel Villanueva, reversing a 2016 dismissal order issued by predecessor Conchita Carpio Morales.

The 2026 Cash Delivery Allegations

The most explosive chapter unfolded in April 2026 during House impeachment proceedings against Vice President Sara Duterte. Self-proclaimed former “bagman” Ramil Madriaga testified under oath that in December 2022, he delivered multiple duffel bags — each containing up to ₱35 million in cash — to the Ombudsman’s parking lot on instructions linked to the Dutertes.

Madriaga claimed the deliveries followed a pattern, with keys left on a red Mazda sedan in the parking lot. He alleged prior deliveries were also made to Martires or Assistant Special Prosecutor Ryan Quilala.

Martires swiftly denied the claims, questioning their logic and lack of direct evidence. Whether proven or not, the testimony has reignited demands for a full investigation into Martires’ tenure.

Legacy: A System Reengineered for Safety?

Martires’ defenders maintain that his policies safeguarded due process and prevented the misuse of anti-graft tools against honest officials.

Yet the cumulative effect — restricted transparency, dismantled investigative tools, and quiet case reversals — created what many analysts describe as an environment where impunity thrived. Corruption cases moved slowly, high-profile allies appeared insulated, and public trust in the Ombudsman eroded.

By the time Martires retired in July 2025, his office had earned a reputation for caution bordering on paralysis in the eyes of reformers. Remulla’s early moves, including lifting SALN restrictions, suggest an institutional course correction.

The deeper question lingers: Can a watchdog that once chose blindness ever fully regain its sight?

In the end, the Ombudsman is not merely an investigator — it is the people’s last line of defense against those who treat public office as private property. When that line blurs into a veil of protection, democracy does not die with fanfare. It fades quietly, one restricted document, one unexamined lifestyle, and one unheeded audit at a time.

True accountability demands more than silence or selective vision. It requires a sentinel unafraid to look directly at power — no matter who wields it.
