Italian Cuisine in the 17th Century

A New World on the Plate

By the 17th century, Italian cuisine had already become one of Europe’s most sophisticated culinary traditions. Marked by regional diversity, an emphasis on fresh seasonal ingredients, and a growing taste for elegant presentation, food culture across the Italian peninsula was entering a period of consolidation. What had once been a collection of city‑state and regional tables was slowly becoming recognizable as a broader “Italian” gastronomic identity. At the same time, the conquest of the Americas—completed in the decades after Columbus’s first voyage—had set off a quiet but profound ingredient revolution. New World crops such as tomatoes, maize, potatoes, chili peppers, and cocoa began to appear in Italian kitchens, gradually altering everyday dishes, social customs, and the geography of cultivation and trade. This article explores how 17th‑century Italian cuisine evolved and how the Americas’ produce quietly reshaped the peninsula’s way of eating.

The 17th‑Century Italian Table

In the early 1600s, Italy was still politically fragmented into a patchwork of duchies, republics, and kingdoms, each with its own culinary style. Tuscan bean soups, Roman pasta dishes, Venetian fish stews, and Neapolitan bread‑based meals coexisted, yet shared a common concern for balance, freshness, and frugality at the everyday level. The typical meal for most Italians remained simple: bread, pulses, vegetables, and olive oil formed the backbone of the diet, with meat and cheese reserved mainly for special occasions or the tables of the wealthy.

At elite courts, however, the 17th century witnessed a refinement of Renaissance manners and service. Banquets became more structured, with an increasing emphasis on courses, tableware, and polite conversation. Cooking manuals also began to shift from aristocratic excess to practical guidance, moving toward the idea of cuisine as both art and science. Many of these texts reflected the lavish meals of the nobility more than the peasant table, as war, plagues, and economic instability meant that food shortages were common. Yet it is precisely in this gap between elite display and popular subsistence that the arrival of New World ingredients began to make a deep, lasting impact.

The Colonial Link: How America Reached Italy

The 1492 “discovery” of the Americas opened a transatlantic network that flowed not only silver, sugar, and slaves but also a stream of plants previously unknown to Europe. Tomatoes, maize, potatoes, chili peppers, beans, pumpkins, and cocoa were among the most important of these. For Italy, access to these goods came indirectly: through Spanish and Portuguese trade routes, via ports such as Naples, Genoa, Livorno, and Palermo, and sometimes through the intermediation of other Mediterranean powers.

By the late 16th century, some of these crops were already being cultivated experimentally in southern Italy and in the papal states, but it was the 17th century that saw their first real integration into Italian agriculture and cookery. The Neapolitan and Sicilian regions, long‑established as hubs of Mediterranean trade, were especially important in this process. Maize spread quickly as a cheap grain for the poor, while tomatoes and chili peppers began to find their way into the kitchens of fishermen, market vendors, and small household cooks. This marked the beginning of a quiet transformation in what Italians considered staple food.

Maize and Polenta: Filling the Bread Gap

One of the most immediate 17th‑century changes was the arrival of maize (Zea mays). Indigenous to the Americas, maize had the advantage of being fast‑growing, relatively drought‑resistant, and highly productive per acre compared with wheat or other Old World cereals. In famine‑prone regions, especially in northern and central Italy, maize quickly became a staple alternative when wheat harvests failed.

By the mid‑ to late 1600s, maize porridge—known as polenta—was becoming a standard dish in Lombardia, the Veneto, and parts of Emilia‑Romagna. Made from coarsely ground maize flour cooked into a thick paste and then sliced or served soft, polenta could be eaten alone in times of scarcity or enriched with cheese, lard, or small amounts of meat when available. This was not merely a matter of taste; it was a subterranean shift in the grain economy. As wheat prices fluctuated and land became more fully exploited, maize helped cushion the rural poor against hunger and allowed families to survive on less bread and more “emergency” grain.

The cultural impact of polenta was subtle but long‑lasting. In many regions, it became the defining peasant food, associated with rural life and simplicity. Over time, it absorbed local flavors—butter and cheese in the north, tomato and herbs in the central‑southern areas—so that by the 18th and 19th centuries polenta had become a classic Italian dish rather than a foreign import.

Tomatoes: From Suspicious Fruit to National Icon

If maize was the quiet workhorse of the 17th‑century Italian table, the tomato was its more dramatic newcomer. The tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) originated in Mesoamerica and arrived in Europe via Spanish ports, then spread slowly through the Mediterranean. Italians were initially wary: the tomato resembled poisonous nightshades, and its bright red color seemed suspicious to many.

For much of the early 17th century, tomatoes were regarded more as ornamental or medicinal plants than as food. Recipes from the period often mentioned them as curiosities rather than staples. Yet in the south—especially in Naples and Sicily—cooks began to experiment with them, drawn by their intense color and acidity. By the late 17th century, simple preparations such as chopped tomatoes tossed with pasta or spread over bread were already appearing in the cuisine of coastal communities.

The real breakthrough came when the tomato was paired with dried pasta. In Trapani and later Naples, longshoremen and fishermen are said to have boiled pasta, drained it, and then topped it with chopped tomatoes and a little olive oil or lard. This simple dish—early pasta al pomodoro—capitalized on ingredients that were relatively cheap: dried semolina pasta, which could be stored for months, and fresh tomatoes, which grew abundantly in the Mediterranean climate. Over time, cooks began to cook tomatoes longer, creating a thicker sauce known as pummarola in Naples.

By the 18th century this combination had become a staple of southern Italian cuisine, and by the 19th it had spread northward, helped by the growing rail network and the publication of practical recipe books for the middle class. The 17th century, therefore, was the crucial incubation period in which the tomato slowly shifted from suspicious foreign oddity to a beloved national ingredient.

Chili Peppers and the Heat of the South

Another American import that began to reshape Italian flavor profiles was the chili pepper. Chili peppers, native to the Americas, came to Europe in various forms, from small dried pods to seeds for cultivation. In Italy, they were particularly embraced in the south, especially in Calabria, Basilicata, and Sicily, where the climate was suitable and the local taste already inclined toward strong, spicy flavors.

By the 17th century, chili peppers were being used to season fish, preserved meats, and vegetable dishes. They provided a cheap and potent way to enliven otherwise simple meals of bread, pasta, and pulses. Dried peppers could be ground into powder or preserved in oil, making them convenient for rural households and seafaring communities alike. Over time, this ingredient gave rise to some of Italy’s most distinctive regional specialties, such as Calabrian ‘nduja and spicy tomato sauces that still define southern cooking.

The introduction of chili peppers also illustrates how the Americas altered the sensory landscape of Italian cuisine. Before the 17th century, heat in Italian food came mainly from black pepper, mustard, and garlic, but the new peppers added a sharper, more lingering burn that reshaped the palate of entire regions.

Potatoes, Cocoa, and Other American Imports

While maize, tomatoes, and chili peppers had the most visible impact on daily meals, other New World ingredients also entered Italian kitchens in the 17th century. The potato, for example, was initially received with great skepticism. Seen as a strange underground tuber, it was often regarded as food fit only for animals or the very poor. Yet by the late 1600s and early 1700s, some northern Italian farmers began to experiment with potatoes as a supplementary crop, especially in less fertile soils where grains struggled.

The potato’s real diffusion into Italian cuisine came later, in the 18th and 19th centuries, when population growth and food insecurity pushed it into mainstream use. By then potatoes were appearing in soups, stuffings, and even as a side dish to pasta and meat, but the groundwork for this shift was laid in the 17th‑century attitude toward American tubers as potential lifelines in times of scarcity.

Cocoa, another American gift, took a different route. Initially reaching Italy through Spanish and Portuguese trade, it was consumed at first as a luxury drink for the wealthy, often mixed with sugar and spices. By the 17th century, chocolate houses and courtly confections began to appear, particularly in northern Italian cities such as Turin, which would later become famous for its chocolate‑based desserts. Unlike tomatoes or maize, cocoa did not enter the everyday peasant diet, but it helped shape the growing culture of Italian sweets and pastries, complementing traditional ingredients like sugar, eggs, and nuts.

Regional Diversification and New Culinary Texts

In the 17th century, Italian cuisine did not become more uniform; instead, regional identities strengthened even as new ingredients arrived. Southern Italy, with its warm climate and access to Mediterranean ports, was especially quick to adopt tomatoes, chili peppers, and maize. Northern Italy, with its colder climate and stronger grain‑based traditions, leaned more on potatoes and maize used in polenta and stews.

This period also saw the rise of explicitly regional cookbooks, which documented how local cooks adapted American ingredients. In 1634, for example, a Neapolitan author published La Lucerna de Corteggiani, a pioneering work that cataloged the seasonal foods of southern Italy—cheeses, olives, cured meats, and fruits—within a framework of regional pride. Later, Antonio Latini produced a multi‑volume recipe collection that centered on the Kingdom of Naples and its rich produce, including the use of tomatoes and other New World goods.

These texts mark a turning point: no longer were cookbooks concerned only with courtly display; they were becoming tools for preserving and transmitting local culinary knowledge. In the process, they helped fix the place of American ingredients within Italian cooking, giving them a home in specific dishes, regions, and seasons.

Social and Cultural Consequences

The arrival of New World ingredients did more than change recipes; it altered social structures and cultural habits. Maize and potatoes, for instance, allowed poorer households to produce more calories from limited land, which in turn supported modest population growth and shifted rural economies. At the same time, the tomato’s rise in the south helped create iconic dishes that later became symbols of national identity—such as simple pasta with tomato sauce and, in later centuries, Neapolitan pizza—even though those dishes only solidified in the 18th and 19th centuries.

The 17th century, therefore, was a period of quiet but profound transformation. Italian cuisine absorbed American ingredients not all at once, but gradually, through experimentation, suspicion, adaptation, and eventual enthusiasm. What began as exotic curiosities ended up as cornerstones of the national diet, reshaping the way Italians farmed, cooked, and thought about food. By the time the 18th century arrived, the Italian table was already becoming recognizably “modern”—rooted in Old World techniques but flavored by the fruits of a distant, conquered continent.
