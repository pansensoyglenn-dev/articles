The Island Tax: Why Philippine Logistics is the Most Expensive in Asia

The Island Tax

Why Philippine Logistics is the Most Expensive in Asia

The Philippines is a maritime nation by definition, comprising over 7,641 islands. In a healthy economy, the sea should be a "blue highway"—a low-cost, high-efficiency route for trade. However, for the average Filipino consumer, the sea has become a barrier. Moving a container of rice from Isabela to Zamboanga shouldn't cost more than importing electronics from South Korea, yet it frequently does.

Understanding the Cabotage Law

The term "Cabotage" refers to the right to operate transport services within a particular territory. In the Philippines, the Tariff and Customs Code and the Public Service Act historically restricted domestic shipping to Philippine-flagged vessels manned by Filipino crews.

While the Foreign Vessels Co-Loading Act (RA 10668) was passed in 2015 to allow foreign ships to carry import/export cargo between local ports, it did not fully liberalize "domestic-to-domestic" trade. Local goods (e.g., corn from Mindanao intended for Manila) must still use local lines, keeping the domestic market insulated from global competition.

The Logistics Paradox

Data from various logistics studies in Southeast Asia consistently show that the Philippines has some of the highest domestic shipping costs in the region. Check the comparison below:

The Three Pillars of the "Shipping Cartel"

1. Lack of Economies of Scale

Because foreign mega-ships (which can carry 10,000+ containers) are banned from domestic routes, we rely on smaller, older Filipino vessels. These smaller ships cannot match the cost-efficiency of global carriers, leading to a higher price-per-container.

2. Aging Infrastructure

The average age of the Philippine domestic fleet is nearly 25 years. These older vessels are less fuel-efficient and require more maintenance. These operational inefficiencies are not absorbed by the companies; they are passed directly to you at the grocery store.

3. Port Monopolies and Fees

Beyond the ships themselves, port handling charges in the Philippines are among the highest in ASEAN. Without competition at the pier, there is no pressure to modernize equipment or reduce bureaucratic fees that plague our supply chain.

The Human Cost: From Farm to Table

When logistics costs are high, both the producer and the consumer lose. This is a regressive reality that hits the poorest Filipinos the hardest:

  • For the Farmer: A corn farmer in Bukidnon receives a lower price for his crop because the middleman has to account for the massive cost of shipping that corn to feed mills in Luzon.
  • For the Family: A mother in Cebu pays a 15-20% markup on basic construction materials or canned goods specifically to cover the "Island Tax."

The Path Forward: Liberalization

To dismantle the shipping oligopoly and lower prices, three critical reforms are needed:

  • Full Liberalization: Allowing 100% foreign ownership in domestic shipping to force local players to modernize.
  • Port Authority Reform: Separating the regulator and operator functions of the PPA to eliminate conflicts of interest.
  • RoRo Modernization: Investing in the Nautical Highway to reduce cargo handling and "double-handling" costs.
