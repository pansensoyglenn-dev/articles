The Great Lobster Rebrand

How the "Sea Roach" of the Atlantic became a $60 dinner staple.

History loves to rewrite the script on what's "luxury." Back in 1622, Plymouth Plantation's Governor William Bradford was mortified to offer arriving guests nothing but lobster and some sketchy water. To him, it screamed poor hosting, not a banquet. Jump to now, and that same "embarrassment" would cost you $60 or more.

From Trash Heap to Table Star

When Europeans hit New England shores, lobsters were everywhere—a total pest. Post-storm beaches supposedly piled up with them two feet deep. So cheap and easy to snag, they turned into classic poor-man's fare: fertilizer for gardens, bait for fish, and basic rations for inmates, indentured workers, and kids.

The Shame of the Shell

  • 1630s: Locals griped that lobsters' abundance made them undesirable eats.
  • 1870s: Lobster shells littering a yard signaled rock-bottom poverty and disgrace.

Railroads Rewrite the Rules

Lobster's big break? Not better taste, but the railroad boom. Mid-1800s train operators, pinching pennies, grabbed cheap lobster, iced it down, and dished it to folks traveling inland.

These landlubbers were ripe for the ploy—they had zero clue about coastal disdain. To them, it felt like a novel, seaside treat: fresh, fancy, and flavorful. Ignorance of the "hate it" rule? Total win.

Sealing the Status Shift

By the 1880s, lobster hit upscale spots in New York and Boston. Demand exploded, and those endless supplies started dwindling. Scarcity kicked in, locking down its elite vibe. Pricey plus delicious? That's prestige that lasts.

In roughly 50 years, it flipped from jail slop to celeb staple—one of history's slickest image overhauls, thanks to rails, distance, and oblivious eaters.

Delicacy or "Sea Spider"?

Biologically, they're oversized aquatic kin to bugs (arthropod squad). Culturally, though? That red shell screams celebration. I’d call it a delicacy with killer branding.
