The Diploma That Gathers Dust

Glenn Junsay Pansensoy April 3, 2026 4 min read
Advertisement

I’ve noticed a heartbreaking trend among Filipinos abroad: they carry two things in their bags—a passport and a diploma that’s gathering dust.

It’s not for lack of brains or grit. It’s because the global market doesn’t give a damn about the seal on that piece of paper if it doesn’t solve a local problem.

The Reality in the Hallway

I’ll never forget a guy I met on a cruise ship. He had a Bachelor’s in Psychology. He was sharp, well-spoken, and incredibly humble. We talked while he was busy mopping a bathroom floor.

He wasn’t bitter. He didn't look down. He just looked at me and said, "Sir, trabaho lang. Kailangan." (Sir, it’s just a job. It’s necessary.)

That line hit me harder than the smell of the cleaning chemicals. He wasn't some isolated case; he was the blueprint for millions. The Philippines is a factory for degrees, but other countries are the factories for opportunities. So, we leave.

The Skills We Look Down On

Here is the uncomfortable truth we hate to say out loud: The jobs that actually survive the AI revolution—the ones that let you build a life without begging for a 9-to-5 or a corporate cubicle—are the very ones we treat as "low class."

We need to stop snubbing the:

  • Electricians and Welders
  • Pandays and Plumbers
  • Mechanics (in a country with millions of bikes!)
  • AC Technicians and Heavy Equipment Operators

You can’t automate a leaky pipe. You can’t outsource a short circuit. These skills don't just "get you by"; they give you the power to be your own boss. They feed families. They build real wealth.

The Education Trap

Instead of promoting trades, we push "mass-produced" degrees—the kind private universities churn out like sacks of rice. We’re paying for lessons that could be learned for free on YouTube, making the schools rich while the students stay broke.

Education stopped being about enlightenment and became a commodity. We bought into the lie that a title equals dignity.

Then reality sets in:

We hang that expensive frame on the wall, fly to Hong Kong or Dubai, and end up scrubbing floors or working as domestic help. We call it "success" because the remittance is high, but deep down, we know the math doesn't add up.

Chasing Prestige Over Practicality

My own journey taught me a hard lesson: A degree is only as valuable as the demand for it. Skills, however, are a universal currency.

The failure isn't the Filipino worker. The failure is a system that conditioned us to chase:

  • Titles instead of Technique.
  • Prestige instead of Practicality.
  • Diplomas instead of Dignity.

We are paying a heavy price for this obsession—one unused, framed piece of paper at a time.
