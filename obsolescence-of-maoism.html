The Obsolescence of Maoism: A Marxist Critique of the CPP-NPA-NDF amid Chinese Imperialist Ascendancy

Marxism insists on ideology rooted in the concrete analysis of concrete conditions. However, the Maoist "protracted people's war" presumes a semi-feudal society where peasants encircle cities—a strategy the Communist Party of the Philippines (CPP), New People's Army (NPA), and National Democratic Front (NDF) have clung to since 1968. After nearly six decades, this peasant-centric dogma has failed to spark a national insurrection, revealing its disconnect from the Philippines' actual political economy.

This mismatch arises from importing tactics from 1930s warlord China into a 21st-century archipelago reshaped by peripheral capitalism. While CPP ideologues mistake capitalist inequality for feudalism, historical materialism shows that the Philippines transitioned to capitalism post-World War II, rendering rural siege tactics a relic.

The Philippines as Peripheral Capitalism, Not Feudalism

True feudalism hinges on extra-economic coercion, such as serfdom. Today’s Philippine countryside is instead a capitalist periphery integrated into global markets.

  • Agribusiness and Proletarianization: Rice production relies on capitalist inputs like mechanized irrigation and wage labor. 2025 data shows 72% of farm workers are casual proletarians earning P350–600 daily, vulnerable to global price cycles rather than bound to lords.
  • De-Agrarianization: Agriculture’s GDP share shrank to 8.2% in 2025, with 62% of labor in urban services and industry. Remittances from 10.5 million Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs), totaling P2.8 trillion in 2025, link rural households directly to international finance capital.
  • Imperial Integration: Large estates like Hacienda Luisita persist as capitalist enclaves for corporations like San Miguel, exemplifying uneven development rather than feudal dominance.

Geopolitical Realities: Chinese Imperialism as the Primary Contradiction

Maoism’s outdated anti-U.S. obsession blinds the CPP-NDF to a critical dialectic: China has eclipsed the U.S. as the primary imperialist threat to Philippine sovereignty.

  • Economic Stranglehold: China controls 40% of Philippine trade. Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) loans, projected to reach P1.2 trillion in debt by 2026, fund projects like the Kaliwa Dam, extracting superprofits via state-owned firms.
  • Military Encroachment: 2026 gray-zone aggressions in the West Philippine Sea block access to an Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) worth P1 trillion annually, impoverishing 1.5 million fisherfolk.
  • Comprador Pivot: Philippine elites now link major infrastructure deals to Beijing. While U.S. bases remain, Chinese finance capital is the ascendant force—the principal contradiction Maoism fails to address.

Peasants Uncaptivated: Class Pragmatism vs. Maoist Romanticism

Maoism posits peasants as the natural revolutionary vanguard, but empirical evidence suggests otherwise.

  • Declining NPA Strength: From a peak of 26,000 in 1987, the NPA has dwindled to roughly 1,200 fighters in 2026. Support is minimal; 2025 polls show CPP sway under 4% in traditional rice belts like Central Luzon.
  • Pragmatic Integration: Smallholders now form cooperatives contracting with firms like Jollibee for steady income. NPA "revolutionary taxes" (P10,000–50,000 per hectare) often provoke backlash rather than solidarity.
  • Generational Shifts: A 2026 survey reveals 68% of rural youth prefer migration or BPO jobs over insurgency. The peasantry—fragmented into smallholders—lacks cohesion without modern proletarian leadership.

The Path to Dialectical Renewal

Maoism’s crisis is a result of its own sectarianism and "China-blindness." By rejecting alliances and boycotting elections, the CPP-NDF has marginalized itself against rising modern contradictions, such as AI-driven joblessness and climate disasters.

A modern Marxist alternative must center on proletarian hegemony. This means uniting factory workers, OFWs, fisherfolk, and the urban poor against both the bureaucrat-capitalist state and Chinese imperialism. Revolution in the 21st century will ignite via strikes, port blockades, and digital mobilization—not rural encirclement. The Philippine proletariat, forged in global export platforms, holds the true revolutionary potential that a fossilized Maoism cannot lead.
