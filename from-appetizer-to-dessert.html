From Eggs to Apples: The Roman Origins of the Modern Multi-Course Meal

When you sit down for a multi-course dinner, beginning with a savory appetizer and concluding with a sweet treat, you are participating in a culinary tradition established by the Romans over two millennia ago. This sequence has remained the gold standard of dining, largely unchallenged by the passage of time.

The Roman Blueprint

The Romans captured this structure in the phrase ab ovo usque ad mala—literally, "from eggs to apples." Just as we might say "from soup to nuts" today, this idiom defined the boundaries of a proper Roman feast:

  • The Gustatio (The Start): The meal opened with light appetizers, almost always featuring eggs.
  • The Main Body: The middle of the dinner was a whirlwind of meats, seafood, complex sauces, vegetables, and wine, all accompanied by lively entertainment and debate.
  • The Mensa Secunda (The Finish): The "second tables" served as the finale, consisting of fresh fruits like pears, figs, or apples.

A Proverbial Standard

By the time the poet Horace used the phrase in his Satires around 35 BC, the "eggs to apples" format was already so deeply ingrained in society that it required no explanation. It wasn't just a trend; it was a proverb. While the exact moment the Romans decided this was the "correct" way to eat is lost to history, it was clearly a settled matter of culture long before it was ever committed to parchment.

The Logic of the Meal

What is truly remarkable is the endurance of this "grammar of eating." Nearly every culture influenced by European culinary roots adheres to this same psychological arc:

  1. Progression: Moving from light and savory to rich and heavy.
  2. Resolution: Ending with a sweet note to signal the conclusion.

We haven't "improved" on this ancient Roman logic because it feels inherently right. We don't view starting with savory and ending with sweet as a choice made by people who lived 2,000 years ago; we view it as common sense. Yet, every time we save dessert for last, we are honoring a rigid structural rule that has survived the fall of empires.

Ab ovo usque ad mala.
From the first bite to the last, the character of the meal must transform, building toward a sweet resolution that has remained unchanged for twenty centuries.
