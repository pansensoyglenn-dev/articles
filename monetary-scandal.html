The Money That Won’t Speak

How Sara Duterte’s Paper Trail Collapses Into Silence

In the shadowed corridors of Philippine power, where public money is meant to flow like a clear river toward the people’s needs, a different current has been running—one that slips through the cracks of ledgers and vanishes into silence.

This is the story of Sara Duterte, Vice President and former Education Secretary, and the confidential funds entrusted to her offices. What began as entries on official documents has morphed into a riddle: If every peso was spent legitimately, why does the trail refuse to add up? Why does it dissolve into ghost names, whispered cash handoffs, and an unyielding quiet where answers should echo?

The Funds Arrive

It all started in late 2022, when the Office of the Vice President (OVP) received a ₱125 million slice of confidential and intelligence funds, pulled from the national government’s contingent pot. The Department of Budget and Management released it under Special Provision No. 1, with the blessing of the Office of the President.

Legally, the moment those funds touched the OVP’s hands, the weight of accountability settled squarely on Sara Duterte’s shoulders. No more shifting blame upward. The buck stopped with her.

Velocity Without Oversight

Yet what followed defied the slow, deliberate rhythm of intelligence work. Officially, the entire ₱125 million was liquidated in just eleven days, right as December drew to a close. Intelligence operations demand planning, verification, and ironclad records—not the speed of a sprint.

Then came the sworn testimony that sharpened the unease. On April 14, 2026, before the House justice committee weighing impeachment complaints, Ramil Madriaga—a former aide now detained in an unrelated case—stepped forward. Under oath, he painted a far more frantic picture: the money had been disposed of in less than twenty-four hours, perhaps as little as eighteen. Bags stuffed with cash—up to ₱35 million apiece—were ferried by security personnel to drop points across the city, including a public parking lot near the Office of the Ombudsman. No ledgers lingered. No electronic footprints marked the path.

The Commission on Audit Steps In

The Commission on Audit (COA) stepped in like a cautious accountant peering over the books. Their review disallowed ₱73.28 million—more than half the original sum—for glaring documentation failures.

Among the red flags were names that seemed plucked from thin air. One stood out: “Mary Grace Piattos.” The Philippine Statistics Authority searched its records and found nothing—no birth certificate, no marriage, no trace of life. It was as if the recipient had been invented from the names of a popular café chain and a beloved snack brand. Other entries raised similar doubts, suggesting payments funneled to phantoms rather than real people carrying out real operations.

COA later upheld the disallowance, ordering Duterte and key OVP officials to return the funds. Her petition for review was denied.

A Larger Shadow: ₱612.5 Million

Zoom out, and the canvas widens. Between 2022 and 2023, the OVP and the Department of Education—both under Duterte’s watch—together commanded at least ₱612.5 million in confidential funds. These were sums shielded by design for sensitive work, yet limited secrecy is not the same as zero oversight.

According to later complaints, the funds became the subject of irregular vouchers, certifications signed without proper review, and cash withdrawn and handed off in ways that left no clear trail.

The Testimony That Lingers

Madriaga’s testimony added layers of shadow. He spoke not only of the 2022 sprint but of deeper patterns reaching back to 2020—allegations of nearly ₱1 billion in cash moved outside the banking system, evading the Anti-Money Laundering Council’s gaze. There were claims of ₱175 million in campaign cash from a figure tied to the Pharmally scandal, another ₱100 million from other donors, and ₱80 million passed through a simple “drop-and-go” in a parking lot.

These were not the disciplined transfers of government bureaucracy; they were bundles loaded into vehicles and delivered privately, never touching a SALN declaration or an audited ledger. Madriaga even submitted a bank secrecy waiver of his own, inviting scrutiny of his records. Duterte has denied knowing him, calling the claims part of a larger plot.

When Silence Becomes the Loudest Reply

Through it all, the response from the Vice President’s camp has been a masterclass in absence. Sara Duterte has skipped key congressional hearings, declined to answer directly under oath, and offered no bank waiver that could slice through the fog in a single gesture.

When pressed in budget hearings, she has pivoted: “I cannot discuss intelligence operations” without compromising national security. Former Supreme Court Senior Associate Justice Antonio Carpio has been blunt—the Senate, sitting as an impeachment court, holds the power to subpoena bank records. Yet no such records have surfaced publicly.

The Painful Contrast

Meanwhile, the contrast stings. In the Department of Education, classrooms remain cramped, teachers juggle shortages of books and supplies, and students make do with whatever scraps are available. Confidential funds were justified as tools for security or urgent aid, yet no landmark intelligence victories or visible public deliverables have been paraded to justify the scale.

By 2024, Congress had stripped both the OVP and DepEd of such funds entirely—a quiet rebuke to the opacity that had reigned.

Accountability Is Not Vengeance

Duterte’s defenders call it politics, a vendetta dressed as oversight. But accountability is not vengeance—it is the bare minimum a democracy demands of those who hold the purse strings.

The truth remains brutally straightforward: clean money leaves footprints in banks, receipts, and public records. It can be traced, shown, and defended. When it cannot, the numbers themselves begin to speak—through disallowed millions, phantom names, rapid cash movements, and the persistent refusal to open the books.

Until a subpoena forces the records into the light, the money that refuses to speak will keep telling its own story. It whispers in every unaudited peso, every ghost receipt, and every unanswered question. And in the Philippines, where public trust is already a fragile thing, that silence carries the weight of billions.
