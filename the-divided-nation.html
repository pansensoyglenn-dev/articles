The Divided Nation

Glenn Junsay Pansensoy · May 1, 2026 · 8 min read · History
The betrayal by our kin
The Fractured Archipelago | A Heartbreak Written in Gold & Iron

The story of the Philippines
is a heartbreak written in gold and iron.

⋆ a fracture calcified into a legacy of systemic betrayal ⋆
— An essay on colonial fracture, kinship lost, and the long reckoning —
Ⅰ • before the shadow

The Harmony of the Archipelago

Before the shadow of the cross fell upon our shores, the islands breathed in unison. Our ancestors were masters of the currents, their balangays weaving a tapestry of connection across the Sulu Sea and beyond. This was a world where gold was worn not just as wealth, but as a testament to craftsmanship, and where the babaylan’s chant reminded every soul that they were part of a sacred whole. The bayanihan spirit wasn’t a slogan; it was the literal movement of a community lifting one another’s burdens. There was no "us" and "them," only the shared rhythm of the tides and the communal wisdom of the datu.

balangay babaylan bayanihan datu Sulu Sea
The sea united what empire later divided — a truth buried beneath galleon timbers.
Ⅱ • galleons & scalpels

The Great Disruption

When the Spanish galleons arrived, they did more than bring new gods and trade routes; they brought a scalpel that sliced through the heart of our communal identity. The Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade turned the archipelago into a global jewel, but it was a jewel polished with the sweat of forced labor and the tears of families torn apart by polo y servicios. As stone walls rose in Intramuros, invisible walls rose between the people. The land, once held in common trust by the spirits and the community, was carved into encomiendas—legal cages that transformed free men into subjects and ancestral domains into private estates.

⚔️ “The earth remembers when it was not owned, only loved. Then came the cross and the ledger.”

The spiritual and territorial rupture was absolute. Where villages once breathed reciprocity, the colonial machinery introduced tribute, conscription, and the hierarchy of race and faith. A wound that would refuse to scar.

Ⅲ • the cruelest irony

The Betrayal of Kinship

The cruelest irony of our history is the birth of the principalia. To maintain control, the colonizers did not just use force; they used temptation. They offered the old chieftains a seat at the table of the oppressor. These former guardians of the barangay became the cabezas and gobernadorcillos—the "little brown brothers" who traded the protection of their people for a Spanish title and a fraction of the plunder.

This was the moment the bayanihan spirit began to wither. The very people who should have led the resistance instead became the tax collectors, the labor wardens, and the enforcers of friar-rule. This intra-community predation created a psychic wound that has never truly closed: the realization that one’s own neighbor, one’s own blood, could be the instrument of one’s misery.

Kinship became a contract signed with a colonizer’s quill. The sword didn’t just cut flesh — it taught our own hands to hold the hilt.
Ⅳ • afterlives of empire

The Legacy: A House Divided

Today, the echoes of this betrayal resonate in every corner of the Republic. The principalia of the 18th century are the political dynasties of 2026. The haciendas that once swallowed communal lands have evolved into sprawling corporate empires and gated communities that look down upon the "informal settlers" from glass towers.

⛓️ This is the current legacy: a society where wealth is a fortress and poverty is a hereditary sentence. We see it in the "trapos" who treat the national treasury as a private galleon, and in a system where the law is a spiderweb—trapping the small while the great tear through it. The communal trust that once defined our ancestors has been replaced by a weary cynicism. We are a nation of 7,000 islands, yet the deepest waters between us are the ones we dug ourselves during centuries of colonial conditioning.

To walk through Manila is to witness this geological sediment of betrayal: the monuments to friars, the avenues named after mestizo elites, the hollowed out plazas where bayani statues cast long shadows over hungry children. The fracture has become architecture, law, and blood memory.

Ⅴ • beautiful, fractured mirror

The Path to Reckoning

To look at the Philippines today is to see a beautiful, fractured mirror. We still dance at the fiesta, and we still claim the bayanihan spirit in times of disaster, but the underlying structure remains broken. The divide is not just an economic statistic; it is an emotional ache—the feeling of being a stranger in one’s own land, ruled by those who share our name but not our struggles.

🌀 Healing this legacy requires more than just policy; it requires a radical reclamation of our pre-colonial wholeness. It demands that we dismantle the dynastic strangleholds that were born in the shadow of the convent. Only by recognizing that our current divisions are the ghosts of colonial "kin-slaying" can we finally begin to bridge the chasm and build a nation where the sea unites us once again.

The gold that once glinted on our ancestors' chests still lies beneath the soil of Mindanao, in the rice terraces of Ifugao, in the memory of every barangay that refused to forget the old name of solidarity. The question is whether we will turn it into a new ornament of power, or melt it into a bridge.

"Only by exhuming the ghost of the principalia can we stop electing them."
— unknown poet of the contemporary reckoning
