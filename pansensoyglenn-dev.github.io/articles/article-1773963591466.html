Egypt’s Living Incubators: Ancient Egg Ovens That Still Work Today

Glenn Junsay Pansensoy 2026-03-20 4 min read

A quiet technological marvel that ran almost continuously for over two millennia — and roughly 200 of them still operate today.

The egg ovens emerged during a period when Egypt was the agricultural engine of the Mediterranean, and poultry was a crucial protein source for both rural families and urban markets. By the 4th century BC, Egypt had already mastered large‑scale food production, irrigation, and seasonal planning, so these brick incubators fit naturally into a culture obsessed with controlling nature for survival.

Ancient writers like Diodorus Siculus marveled at the system, noting that Egyptians could hatch chicks without hens, a feat no other civilization had achieved at such scale. The ovens became a quiet technological marvel, running almost continuously during the spring season to meet the empire’s demand for poultry.

What makes them even more extraordinary is how they endured through political upheavals, Persian rule, Greek rule, Roman rule, the Islamic caliphates, Ottoman administration, and into the modern era, without ever being formally documented.

Their survival depended entirely on specialized families who guarded the knowledge as a hereditary craft. European travelers from the 1700s onward described the ovens with a mix of awe and confusion, unable to replicate the results despite thermometers and scientific instruments.

The fact that roughly 200 of these incubators still operate today isn’t just a technological legacy, it’s a living example of ancient Egyptian engineering that never broke its chain of transmission.
