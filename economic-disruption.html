The Iran War & Global Trade Disruption

The war in Iran has severely disrupted international trade, primarily through the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, a vital chokepoint for 20% of global oil and significant LNG flows. This has spiked energy prices, rerouted shipping via longer paths like the Cape of Good Hope, and imposed conflict surcharges by carriers. Economies worldwide face higher costs, supply chain delays, and recession risks if disruptions persist.

🛢️ Strait of Hormuz Closure

Iran's blockade has halted roughly 20 million barrels of daily oil transit, driving crude prices above $80 per barrel and potentially to $100–$200. War-risk insurance premiums have surged, deterring tankers and compounding delays with Houthi attacks on the Suez route, affecting one-third of seaborne crude trade.

Businesses now audit Gulf exposures and seek alternative freight amid two-to-four-week lag effects. The cumulative impact threatens to reshape maritime insurance and routing norms for the foreseeable future.

📉 Iran's Economic Toll

Iran's GDP could drop over 10% from disrupted output, postponed investments, and infrastructure damage from strikes on factories, power grids, and ports. Despite sanctions, it maintains some food and parts imports while exporting oil and materials, but ceasefire failures threaten deeper collapse.

Tourism and non-oil sectors suffer most acutely, with domestic consumption sharply contracting and capital flight accelerating.

Key Figure: GDP decline projected at >10% — driven by infrastructure destruction and trade isolation.

🌐 Global Energy Importers

Major importers like China, India, Japan, and the EU bear disproportionate hits from supply shortages and price hikes. China's internalized chains limit some damage but expose its export reliance to slowdowns. Europe's industries, already strained post-Russia sanctions, face 1%+ GDP cuts.

Projections indicate Kuwait and Qatar GDP could shrink by 14%, while Saudi Arabia and the UAE may see contractions of 3–5% if the war extends.

⚠️ Regional Vulnerabilities

Gulf exporters risk output halts dependent on the strait. Europe contends with compounded energy woes from prior Nord Stream attacks. Emerging markets vulnerable to sustained high prices see limited overall GDP drag but sharper local pain.

The WTO warns that prolonged high oil and gas prices could trim global GDP growth by 0.3% in 2026, with the most acute effects concentrated in energy-import-dependent economies.

🚢 Supply Chain Shifts

Unlike COVID's demand shocks, this war targets energy corridors, mirroring tariff-driven changes but hitting commodities harder. Companies suspend Hormuz voyages, inflating global shipping costs. Lasting trade reshaping is expected, with urgent calls for supply chain audits and cyber defenses against Iranian threats.

Logistics networks are being fundamentally redrawn, with diversification away from single-chokepoint dependency becoming a strategic priority.

🇵🇭 Philippine Context

As an import-dependent economy, the Philippines faces elevated fuel and commodity prices, with far-reaching trade ripple effects. Energy costs strain households and industries alike, while global slowdowns pose risks to growth, remittances, and export demand.

Policymakers are monitoring inflationary pressures and considering buffer measures to shield vulnerable sectors from prolonged price volatility.
