Hijacked Hopes

Glenn Junsay Pansensoy · April 27, 2026 · 5 min read · National Affairs
The hijacked hopes
The Filipino Dream Under Siege

The Philippines is a land of breathtaking beauty and heartbreaking contradictions.

It is a nation of resilient smiles that often mask the tears of a people pushed to the edge. Today, the Filipino dream is under siege, and our youth—the very "hope of the Fatherland"—are being forced to trade their pens for protest placards and their classrooms for the streets in a desperate cry for survival.

The Bleeding Wounds of Corruption

Corruption in our country is not just a headline; it is a thief that enters every Filipino home. It lives in the mahogany halls of power and reaches down to the smallest barangay, siphoning the lifeblood of our nation. From the enduring grip of political dynasties like the Marcoses, Dutertes, and Aquinos—who treat public office like a family heirloom—to the billions lost in "pork barrel" scams, the money meant for our children’s future vanishes into the pockets of the few.

While officials flaunt luxury cars and designer clothes, our fellow Filipinos line up before dawn for a bucket of clean water. We see our schools crumble under the first gust of a typhoon because the cement was watered down by greed, and we watch our grandparents die in hospital hallways because the medicine budget was raided for a campaign fund. This is the "culture of impunity" that breaks our hearts: a system where justice is a luxury only the rich can afford.

Youth Activism: A Heartbeat of Defiance

Imagine being a young Filipino today. You study for years, fueled by your parents' sweat and tears, only to graduate into a world that has no place for you. We see engineers serving coffee and lawyers answering phones in call centers because the "economic growth" we hear about on TV never reaches the kitchen table. When the minimum wage cannot even buy a kilo of rice and a decent meal for a family, what is left but rage?

This is why our youth take to the streets. They saw the billions wasted during the pandemic while they struggled with slow internet and rising tuition. They see their parents aging prematurely from overwork. Alyssa Alano and her companions were not "rebels" by choice; they were brilliant souls who loved this country too much to watch it die. When the state "extinguishes" these bright lights through violence or neglect, it isn't just killing individuals—it is killing the future of the Philippines. Activism isn't a crime; it is the ultimate act of love for a country that is hurting.

The Misery of the Working Class

Our economy is a hollow shell. We boast of rising numbers while our workers are treated like disposable parts. Through "endo" and contractualization, our youth are hired and fired every six months, denied the dignity of security and benefits. Our farmers, the very people who put food on our tables, are the ones going hungry, earning a pittance while the cost of seeds and fertilizer soars. To be a worker in the Philippines is to be trapped in a cycle of "isang kahig, isang tuka"—one scratch, one peck—where one illness can bankrupt an entire generation.

OFWs: Heroes of a Broken Home

We call our Overseas Filipino Workers "Modern-Day Heroes," but we forget the tragedy behind that title. No mother wants to leave her children to raise someone else's child in a foreign land. No father wants to spend twenty years at sea just to pay for a house he only visits once a year.

Our biggest export is not electronics or coconut oil—it is the Filipino heart. We send our best nurses, teachers, and engineers abroad because their own country has turned its back on them. We celebrate the billions they send back, but we ignore the "broken homes," the children growing up via video calls, and the mental anguish of parents who are strangers to their own families. We are a nation built on the sacrifice of exile.

Farmers: The Forgotten Souls of the Soil

Our farmers are being squeezed to extinction. While we import cheap rice from abroad to satisfy trade deals, our own tillers are buried in debt. They face the fury of nature—the droughts of El Niño and the floods of the monsoons—with almost no help from the government. When they ask for land, they are met with bullets or red-tagging. The hands that feed us are the most calloused and the least rewarded, watching their children flee to the slums of the city because there is no future in the soil.

A Call for a New Morning

The crisis of the Philippines is not an accident; it is a choice made by those in power. But the Filipino spirit is not easily broken. Activism—from the farmers' cooperatives to the student strikes—is the fire that keeps our hope alive. It is the legacy of EDSA, the proof that when Filipinos stand together, even the tallest walls of tyranny can fall.

We dream of a Philippines where a student like Alyssa Alano can graduate and serve her country without fear. We dream of a land where a farmer owns the field he plows, where a mother doesn't have to board a plane to feed her kids, and where "government service" truly means serving the poor.

The struggle is long, and the heart is heavy, but we continue to fight. Because the Philippines is worth the activism. It is worth the protest. It is worth our love.
