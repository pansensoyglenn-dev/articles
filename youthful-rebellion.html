Why Our Youth Take to the Hills:
Stolen Dreams and the Call of Rebellion

A reckoning with silence, sacrifice, and the faces behind the statistics

Essay · Witness · Reflection

A few days ago, in the rugged interior of Negros Occidental, a 22-year-old University of the Philippines student named Alyssa Alano was killed. The military reports an armed encounter with New People's Army rebels; she was among them, they say. The NPA counters that she was a civilian, there for community work. Nineteen dead from her side. Zero military casualties. No survivors to contradict the official line.

I never met Alyssa. I can't say what she carried that day—a rifle, a notebook, or just hope—or what unfolded in those chaotic final minutes. But here's what I do know: When the state's enforcers clash with civilians amid a long history of extrajudicial killings, fabricated encounters, and denied atrocities, the burden of proof doesn't lie with the silenced dead.

It rests with those who pulled the triggers. Heavily.

We're owed more than press briefings and human rights platitudes. We need forensic evidence, witness testimonies, independent investigations—not self-policing reviews that vanish into bureaucracy. Until transparency reigns and accountability stings, skepticism isn't paranoia; it's prudence. The military's presumption of innocence? In these shadows, it evaporates.

Victim-Blaming in the Shadow of Tragedy

Social media swirls with the inevitable: What was she doing there? A UP grad student in a war zone? Reckless. Naive. Maybe she asked for it.

This line of thought isn't just unsettling—it's insidious. It pivots from the violence inflicted to the victim's supposed provocations, echoing age-old excuses for injustice. As evidence stands, Alyssa did nothing criminal. She was a passionate advocate: immersing in farming villages, championing land reform, amplifying labor struggles. Activities that, in a functioning democracy, earn medals, not bullets.

We should celebrate such fire in the young, not condemn it.

“She was a passionate advocate: immersing in farming villages, championing land reform, amplifying labor struggles. Activities that, in a functioning democracy, earn medals, not bullets.”

The Grinding Realities That Breed Rebels

Truth is, Filipino youth don't arm up from whimsy. They're propelled by a toxic brew: joblessness, poverty wages, and corruption that mocks their toil.

Recall a dusty barrio road, years back. A man in his late thirties—calloused hands, weary eyes—claps my shoulder. "Alam mo, maswerte kayo mga kabataan na nagaaral. May kinabukasan kayo. Yung mga anak ko? Hanggang dito na. Magsasaka lang, parang akin." ("You're lucky, studying with futures ahead. My kids? This is their ceiling. Farmers, like me.")

I ached to rebut: Farming's noble! They could rise—doctors, coders, leaders! But silence was wiser. His world was raw fact, mine sheltered theory.

That exchange haunts me, fueling my work. It exposed education's myth as the great leveler—real only if accessible, equitable, unrigged.

Fast-forward to 2026: Official youth unemployment lingers at 12–15% (higher off-books), with 40% underemployed in gigs like tricy-driving or vending for ₱300–400/day. Inflation devours it; a family's rice alone costs half. Meanwhile, corruption feasts: ₱200 billion+ lost yearly to graft, per audits—Pork Barrel 2.0, fake NGOs, dynastic slush funds. Officials and their kin flaunt yachts in Europe, condos in Dubai, built on taxes from the very poor they neglect.

In Negros Occidental, sugar barons and politicians collude: haciendas swallow small farms, workers earn ₱250/day amid hunger marches. No irrigation? Blame ghost projects. No schools? Embezzled budgets. Youth graduate to walang trabaho ("no work") signs, or migrate as OFWs, remitting billions that line elite banks.

Enter the NPA. Not cartoon villains, but symptom. Recruits: dropouts from jobless barrios, grads like Alyssa radicalized by immersion. They see the gap—Manila's malls vs. rural mud—and the armed struggle promises agency. Thousands join yearly, per reports, not from ideology alone, but despair. Low wages chain families; corruption steals escape routes. Rebellion whispers: Fight, or fade.

Alyssa witnessed this abyss. In her campaigns, she met mothers rationing rice, kids out of school for labor, lands contested by goons. Her "recklessness"? A moral compass pointing to the unseen.

Nothing Wrong with Honest Work — Everything Wrong with Forced Paths

No shame in farming, fishing, factory lines, or overseas sweat. Dignity lies in choice. Wrong is circumstance dictating destiny: Born in a sitio, doomed to drudgery while elites hoard.

We glorify OFWs as heroes—rightly—but ignore the push: No local jobs, wages too low to build lives here. Education falters too—overcrowded public schools, 20% dropout rates in poor provinces, elite unis for the connected.

Alyssa's generation rebels not from folly, but fury at this rigged game. They trek to frontlines to listen, organize, humanize the statistics. Critics call it naive; I call it vital.

“Alyssa's generation rebels not from folly, but fury at this rigged game. They trek to frontlines to listen, organize, humanize the statistics.”

The Courage We Dare Not Lose

Challenge their politics? Absolutely — democracy thrives on clash.

But deem their deaths self-inflicted? That's apathy masquerading as wisdom. It absolves the system that starves dreams, corrupt hands that pick pockets, bullets that end questions.

If we shame the young for straying lanes—for asking why taxes build mansions not bridges, why diplomas yield despair—we betray our future.

We kill the instinct to interrogate power.

We dim visions of equity, where wages sustain, jobs abound, graft withers.

We extinguish that reckless, imperfect courage—the kind storming hills to link the fortunate to the forsaken.

It's youth's gift. In Alyssa, it burned bright. Let it light the way, not die in doubt.
