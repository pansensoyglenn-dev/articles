Echoes of the Ancestors:
The Forgotten Spirits Before the Cross

Glenn Junsay Pansensoy April 3, 2026 6 min read Culture · History · Spirituality

Ever wondered what occupied the Filipino soul before the first cathedral was built? Long before the 1521 arrival of Spanish friars, the archipelago didn't have a "Holy Week." There were no wooden crosses being carried through the streets, no Pabasa chanting the Passion, and no town-wide reenactments of Calvary.

Yet, the pre-colonial spiritual landscape was far from empty. In fact, it was a world teeming with rituals so raw and mystical they might leave a modern observer breathless.

The Guardians of the Gateway

The heart of ancient Filipino spirituality wasn't a book, but a person: the Babaylan. These spiritual leaders—predominantly women or feminized men—were the bridge between the physical and the ethereal.

While modern Holy Week centers on the sacrifice of a single deity, the Babaylan’s rituals honored a vast pantheon. During times of communal crisis or seasonal shifts, they led days-long ceremonies that were the true ancestors of our modern religious gatherings. But instead of praying to a distant heaven, they spoke to the trees, the rivers, and the spirits of their own grandparents.

Sacred Offerings and the "Pag-anito"

The closest ancient parallel to the solemnity of Lent was the Pag-anito. This wasn't a time for seeking forgiveness for "sin"—a concept that didn’t exist in the same way back then—but a time for restoring balance.

  • Offerings: Communities presented wine, food, and animal sacrifices to appease the anitos (spirits).
  • The Trance: Unlike the silent meditation of a modern chapel, these rituals involved rhythmic chanting and trance-states, where the soul was believed to travel to other realms.
  • Mourning Taboos: Much like the "Quiet Friday" we know today, ancient Filipinos practiced strict periods of silence and simplicity following a death, believing that their behavior directly influenced the soul’s journey to the afterlife (Sulad or Saad).

A Different Kind of Sacrifice

We often see modern penitents whip themselves in an act of contrition. The ancient Filipino also understood the power of blood and pain, but the motivation was different:

The Great Spiritual Fusion

The "shocking" reality is that the Spanish didn't just replace the old ways; they built over them. This is called syncretism.

When you hear the haunting, melodic wail of the Pabasa today, you are likely hearing an echo of the ancient Babaylan’s oral epics. When you see a community come together in intense, shared grief during a funeral or procession, you are witnessing a social DNA that predates Catholicism by millennia.

Ancient Filipinos didn't need a calendar to tell them when to be holy. They lived in a constant state of spiritual exchange with nature. So, as the Philippines observes its modern traditions, it’s worth remembering: the "Old Ways" aren't gone—they’re just wearing a different mask.

— Glenn Junsay Pansensoy
