The Godfathers of Philippine Politics

In the sun-drenched islands of the Philippines, beneath the swaying palms and the vibrant chaos of everyday life, a darker shadow lingers. It is the shadow of mafia-style governance — where public office has quietly become a throne for modern godfathers, ruling not through laws alone, but through fear, loyalty, private armies, and cold cash.

This is not the Sicily of old, yet the parallels are unmistakable. Political clans treat provinces and cities like personal fiefdoms. They offer protection and favors in exchange for unquestioning support. Cross them, and the consequences can be swift and brutal. Critics call it "godfather politics," and in many parts of the country, especially Mindanao and the Visayas, it feels painfully real. In Soccsksargen, for instance, clans like the Teves in Negros Oriental have extended influence through alliances, blending local patronage with threats that echo across regions.

Historical Foundations

The roots run deep. Long before independence, Spanish colonial haciendas created powerful landed elites — the caciques — who answered to no one but themselves. These landowners controlled vast tracts, using tenant farmers as voting blocs. American rule formalized this through padrinos (godfathers) who rigged elections via balut (buying votes with cash or food). The turbulent post-independence years saw warlords like the Durans in Iloilo or the Lacsons in Negros Occidental consolidate power amid Huk rebellions and banditry.

Then came Ferdinand Marcos and martial law (1972–1986). Private militias flourished under "civilian home defense forces," arming loyalists against communist insurgents. Marcos's cronies, like the Enrile and Romualdez clans, amassed fortunes through crony capitalism. This template for today's political dynasties was set in blood and iron, with over 70,000 extrajudicial killings documented by Amnesty International during that era.

The Dynasty Data

Decades later, little has changed in spirit. Families still dominate the political landscape. A 2022 Ateneo de Manila University study showed that more than 70% of seats in Congress are held by members of political dynasties — up from 60% in 2013. The Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism (PCIJ) mapped over 200 families controlling 80% of gubernatorial posts in 2022. In Davao, the Duterte family has ruled for over four decades; Rodrigo Duterte, the tough-talking former mayor, built his reputation on an iron-fisted approach that many say included shadowy death squads. Now his daughter Sara sits as Vice President, while son Sebastian governs Davao City, carrying forward a legacy that blends charisma, patronage, and raw power.

In the Visayas, the Garcias of Cebu hold a stranglehold, with family members occupying governor, congressman, and mayor roles simultaneously. Mindanao's examples abound: the Dimaporo clan in Lanao del Norte and the Pimentels in Misamis Occidental rotate positions across generations, often unchallenged.

The Muscle: Private Armed Groups

At the heart of this system are the private armed groups (PAGs). Though illegal under Republic Act 9851, these militias continue to operate in the shadows. Before the 2022 elections, the Commission on Elections (Comelec) claimed to have dismantled 4,000+ loose firearms and neutralized 200 PAGs, yet a 2023 Sandiganbayan report estimates 100–200 groups persist, with 20,000–50,000 armed men enforcing patrons' will. They ensure votes through intimidation (e.g., "gun bans" routinely violated), silence critics, and eliminate rivals.

The 2009 Maguindanao Massacre remains the bloodiest reminder: 58 people — including 32 journalists, lawyers, and women — slaughtered by the Ampatuan clan's 100+ gunmen to block rival Esmael Mangudadatu's candidacy. Convictions came in 2023, but Andul Ampatuan Jr. allegedly still pulls strings from prison. Closer to home, the 2023 killing of Negros Oriental Gov. Roel Degamo by alleged Teves hitmen highlights ongoing PAG threats in central Philippines.

Patronage and Economic Strangleholds

Violence is only one tool. The real glue is patronage. In exchange for votes, politicians distribute rice sacks, jobs, cash (walkouts), and promises of protection. A 2024 World Bank report notes that 38% of Filipinos rely on such clientelism, with rural households 50% more likely to vote for patrons providing aid. In poorer provinces like Sultan Kudarat in Soccsksargen, communities depend on clan-led relief during typhoons or crises.

Economic control runs parallel. Dynasties grip local industries — mining (e.g., Tampakan copper-gold in Soccsksargen, contested by Rajah Buayan clan influences), logging, quarrying, and jueteng. The 2013 pork barrel scam (PDAF) siphoned P10 billion into fake NGOs run by dynasties like the Relampagos. Recent exposes reveal Sara Duterte's office spent P1.2 billion in confidential funds (2022–2023) with minimal accounting, fueling "slush fund" accusations.

National Echoes and Global Scrutiny

Rodrigo Duterte's presidency took this model national. His war on drugs left 6,252 dead officially (PNP data), but Human Rights Watch tallies 12,000–30,000, many via vigilantes. The "Davao Death Squad" (linked to 1,400+ killings pre-2016) scaled up, prompting the ICC's 2021 investigation (ongoing despite withdrawal). Under President Ferdinand Marcos Jr., patterns persist: 2025 feuds between Marcos and Duterte allies mirror mafia turf wars, with confidential funds scandals totaling P13 billion across offices.

Pushback and Paths Forward

Yet not everyone accepts this quietly. Civil society like Atin Ito and Sandiganbayan prosecutors, brave journalists at Rappler (facing tax raids), and reform groups like Laban Kontra Dynasty petitioned the Supreme Court in 2023 to enforce the anti-dynasty ban. Comelec's 2025 innovations — AI surveillance drones and blockchain voting pilots — cut violence by 25% in test areas. The 1987 Constitution bans dynasties (Art. II, Sec. 26), but Congress stalls. Grassroots wins, like Bantay-Kontra Dinastiya ousting local bosses in Cebu, show voter shifts.

Poverty (21% rate, PSA 2024), weak institutions, and fear sustain the system. As 2028 nears, tech-savvy youth in Davao and Soccsksargen leverage apps like VoteSmart PH for dynasty trackers. Breaking the cycle demands electoral reforms (e.g., party-list strengthening), anti-PAG enforcement, and economic alternatives like cooperatives.

Philippine democracy has shown resilience before — the 1986 People Power Revolution toppled Marcos with 2 million protesters. Today, it needs outrage plus action: stronger Comelec independence, transparent funding, and voters rejecting patronage. Until then, the archipelago's bright promise remains haunted by its own shadows — where ballots and blood still determine who truly rules.
