The Philippines’ Resource Curse and the Weight of Chinese Imperialism

The Philippines remains one of Asia’s most resource‑rich countries, yet it has failed to industrialize in a way that translates those riches into broad‑based, self‑sustaining development. Despite large nickel, copper, gold, and other mineral deposits, manufacturing has long been weak and is now in relative decline, a process that many Philippine‑based Marxist and anti‑imperialist analyses argue is aggravated by “Chinese imperialism” in trade, finance, and regional geopolitics.

A nation rich in ore, poor in industry

The Philippines sits on enormous nickel reserves and substantial copper, gold, and other metallic and non‑metallic minerals. These resources have long fed raw‑materials exports, yet the country’s manufacturing share of GDP has hovered around low levels, far below more industrialized neighbors.

This “resource curse” reflects not a lack of natural wealth, but decades of policy failure: colonial and post‑colonial structures kept the economy oriented toward export‑crop and raw‑materials extraction rather than value‑added processing. Post‑1986 liberalization slashed tariffs and opened markets to cheap imports, undercutting local industries before they could become competitive. Successive governments produced industrialization “plans” without binding targets, technology‑transfer clauses, or serious protection for infant industries, allowing the economy to drift toward services and low‑end assembly.

As a result, the Philippines exports ores and semi‑processed materials while importing much of its capital goods, electronics, textiles, and machinery—a pattern that deepens underdevelopment rather than breaks it.

De‑industrialization under “new imperialism”

In Marxist and anti‑imperialist frameworks, “imperialism” is not just about formal colonies, but about the domination of weaker economies by powerful capitalist states through unequal exchange, finance, and foreign control over key sectors. The Philippines, long described as a semi‑colony of U.S. imperialism, is now also caught in a rising rivalry between U.S. and Chinese imperial powers, each seeking to lock the country into a subordinate role in global value chains.

From this perspective, Chinese imperialism aggravates de‑industrialization in several concrete ways: market displacement by Chinese goods, integration into unequal value chains, infrastructure‑led dependency without manufacturing leverage, and capital inflows that favor construction and real estate.

China is one of the Philippines’ largest trading partners and a major source of imports, especially in electronics, textiles, machinery, and consumer goods. Cheap Chinese products undercut local manufacturers, making it harder for domestic firms to accumulate capital, scale up, or invest in new technology. Participation in China‑linked supply chains and large infrastructure and trade initiatives often positions the Philippines as a supplier of raw materials or low‑value intermediate products, while final‑value activities remain in China. This repeats the classic pattern of “export raw materials, import manufactures,” which prevents structural upgrading of local industry.

Chinese‑financed or Chinese‑built infrastructure projects—ports, railways, dams, and roads—can boost trade and construction, but they often come with opaque financing, unfavorable loan terms, and limited local content. These projects can create jobs and raise economic activity, but real industrial gains depend on the host state actively using them to push local manufacturing and technology transfer, something the Philippine government has largely failed to do. Moreover, Chinese foreign direct investment into the Philippines has surged under recent administrations, but a large share tilts toward real estate, construction, and resource‑linked projects rather than deep‑cycle manufacturing. This strengthens domestic elites and crony‑type conglomerates that profit from building and land speculation, but do little to restructure the industrial base toward steel, machinery, or advanced electronics.

A dual pattern of dependency

Anti‑imperialist analyses in the Philippines emphasize that the country experiences dual dependency: historical dependency on U.S. and Western capital (through trade, finance, and military‑political ties), which long locked the Philippines into a semi‑colonial, raw‑materials‑export role; and emerging dependency on Chinese capital and trade, which now channels more of the Philippine economy into infrastructure, raw‑materials exports, and low‑value linkages, while reinforcing de‑industrialization trends.

Under this dual‑dependency framework, Chinese imperialism does not “cause” Philippine de‑industrialization by itself, but it aggravates it by: deepening the Philippines’ integration into global value chains that are centered on China’s industrial core; increasing the country’s exposure to debt‑led growth and infrastructure dependence; and crowding out local manufacturing through saturated imports and unequal investment terms.

Toward a program of industrial self‑reliance

To reverse de‑industrialization under this imperialist backdrop, radical and left‑leaning economic analyses in the Philippines call for: a national industrial policy with protection, technology transfer, and local content rules, especially for steel, machinery, and strategic minerals such as nickel and copper; debt and trade sovereignty, including strict scrutiny of infrastructure‑linked loans and projects, with clear conditions that favor domestic job creation, skills development, and industrial upgrading; and reorientation of the resource sector, so that minerals are partially processed domestically and linked to a domestic manufacturing base instead of being exported raw to feed foreign factories.

Without such a deliberate shift, the Philippines risks remaining a semi‑industrialized, resource‑exporting economy whose “growth” is increasingly tied to the rhythms of Chinese and U.S. imperial power—exporting ores, importing manufactures, and watching its industrial base slowly erode.
