The Credibility Deficit: Rhetoric vs. Evidence in Philippine Politics

The Credibility Deficit

Why Rhetoric Cannot Replace Evidence in the Pursuit of Truth
April 18, 2026

In the humid pulse of Manila’s political arena, where the weight of history presses down like an unyielding tropical storm, a single moment on April 15, 2026, cut through the noise like a blade.

Senator Imee Marcos, her voice rising during a press conference that would soon flood social media feeds, turned her fury toward a man she had never met in person but whose words had rattled the corridors of power. “Who you, Madriaga? Sinungaling ka!” she thundered—You are a liar!—dismissing Ramil Madriaga without a shred of counter-proof. Her words echoed not just through the room, but across a nation still haunted by ghosts from its darkest chapter. It was political theater, yes, but beneath the drama lay a deeper fracture: the growing chasm between raw rhetoric and the quiet, unflinching demand for evidence.

Verification in the Face of Accusation

Just one day earlier, on April 14, the House Committee on Justice had convened for another intense session in the impeachment proceedings against Vice President Sara Duterte. The charges—centered on alleged misuse of public funds and betrayal of public trust—had drawn witnesses from the shadows of political dealings.

Among them stood Ramil Madriaga, the self-described former bagman for the vice president. He did what few in this archipelago of guarded secrets dare: he stepped forward with sworn affidavits, a supplemental statement dated just days prior, and a voluntary waiver of bank secrecy laws. Tears welled in his eyes as he read aloud under oath, his voice cracking with the gravity of what he claimed to have witnessed—details of financial flows, alleged deals, and hidden arrangements that he insisted demanded scrutiny.

By signing those documents, Madriaga didn’t just speak. He invited the full machinery of the law to dissect him. Perjury charges loomed if he faltered. Forensic audits of his accounts awaited. Cross-examination would come, relentless and public. In the unforgiving arena of impeachment, this was no casual accusation flung from a keyboard. It was a high-stakes wager on truth—the kind that separates gossip from governance.

The Response: Vituperation Over Evidence

Yet across town, Senator Marcos chose a different path. No affidavits from her side. No waivers. No engagement with the paper trail Madriaga had laid bare. Just the raw force of denial, laced with personal venom.

In the logic of law and reason, such a response isn’t rebuttal—it’s deflection, a shield against the very process designed to safeguard democracy. And in the Philippines, where dynasties rise and fall on the tides of public memory, this moment carried echoes far louder than any press briefing.

The Shadow of Archimedes Trajano

To understand why, one must journey back nearly five decades, to 1977, when the iron grip of Martial Law still choked the nation. Archimedes Trajano was a bright-eyed student activist, the kind who believed questions could change the world. At a public forum, he dared challenge Imee Marcos—then head of the Kabataang Barangay youth group—on her leadership and the regime’s tightening control.

Three days later, he vanished. His body was later discovered, bearing the unmistakable marks of brutal torture: beaten, mangled, broken. A U.S. federal court in Hawaii, years after the Marcos family fled into exile following the 1986 People Power Revolution, would painstakingly examine the evidence. In a landmark ruling, the court found Imee Marcos-Manotoc civilly liable for Trajano’s wrongful death by torture. Judgment was entered based on findings that she controlled the military intelligence personnel responsible. The award: millions in damages to his grieving mother, Agapita. Appeals upheld it.

Though technicalities later shielded her from enforcement in some forums, the judicial record stood firm—a permanent scar on the collective conscience, documented not in partisan whispers but in courtroom transcripts and international human rights archives.

“Filipinos can forgive, but should never forget the atrocities and despotism.”
— Edcel Lagman, veteran lawmaker and Martial Law survivor

This shadow lingers because the Martial Law era, declared in 1972 by Ferdinand Marcos Sr., left behind a documented trail of horror: tens of thousands detained without trial, subjected to electric shocks, waterboarding, and extrajudicial killings. Veteran lawmaker Edcel Lagman has long warned against the “widespread amnesia” that threatens to repeat history.

A Dangerous Asymmetry of Accountability

Today, in 2026, that same pattern risks resurfacing—not through midnight abductions but through the subtler erosion of institutional trust. The impeachment of a sitting vice president is no mere political sideshow. It is a constitutional stress test, enshrined in the 1987 Charter to protect the public from abuses of office.

Madriaga’s testimony, emotional and detailed as it was, doesn’t demand blind belief. It demands the opposite: open the books, subpoena the records, let forensic experts and cross-examiners do their work.

Defending an ally is politics as usual. Shielding them by branding a sworn witness a liar, without evidence, borders on obstruction. It creates an asymmetry where one side risks everything under oath while the other relies on volume and vituperation.

The Stakes for Philippine Democracy

In a democracy still healing from dynastic scars—where families like the Marcoses and Dutertes have shaped presidencies, vice presidencies, and senatorial seats—the public isn’t choosing sides based on loyalty alone. They’re watching whether institutions hold.

Selective amnesia doesn’t just distort the past; it weakens the present. It tells future witnesses: why bother with affidavits when a shout can suffice? It tells citizens: your trust in governance is secondary to the loudest voice in the room.

Yet hope persists in the quiet courage of process. Madriaga’s waiver wasn’t bravado; it was an invitation to truth-seekers. The impeachment committee’s subpoenas, the oaths administered, the public scrutiny—these are the tools of a maturing republic.

History’s long memory, from Trajano’s tortured body to the affidavits now under review, reminds us that power unchecked devours itself.

The real question isn’t “Who are you, Madriaga?”

It is: Who among our leaders is willing to submit their claims to the cold, impartial microscope of evidence?

In a nation forged in revolution against tyranny, the answer should never be shrouded in rhetoric. It must shine under the light of verification—for the sake of every Filipino who remembers, and for those who must never forget.
