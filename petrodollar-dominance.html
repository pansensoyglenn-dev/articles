The Petrodollar System and Its Grip on the Global Economy

How oil priced in dollars shapes currency markets, fuels peso devaluation, and stokes Philippine inflation

The term petrodollar refers to the overwhelming dominance of the U.S. dollar in international oil trade: oil is quoted, invoiced, and usually settled in dollars, and oil‑exporting countries then recycle their surplus “petrodollars” into dollar‑denominated assets such as U.S. Treasuries and bank deposits.[1][2] This petrodollar system emerged in the 1970s after the collapse of the Bretton Woods gold standard, when the United States struck bilateral agreements with key Middle Eastern oil producers—most notably Saudi Arabia—tying their oil sales to the dollar in exchange for security and political backing.[3][4]

Because oil is the world’s most traded commodity, these arrangements effectively locked the global trading system into a dollar‑centric circuit. Every oil‑importing country, including the Philippines, must earn or borrow dollars to pay for its crude and refined‑fuel imports, reinforcing demand for the dollar far beyond the size of the U.S. economy.[1][4] This “exorbitant privilege,” as economists call it, allows the United States to finance large trade and fiscal deficits by issuing debt that the rest of the world is happy to hold, while keeping borrowing costs relatively low.[1][2]

How Petrodollars Shape Currency Markets

Petrodollar recycling has two immediate macroeconomic effects: first, it underpins the dollar’s status as the world’s primary reserve and invoicing currency; second, it amplifies the sensitivity of non‑dollar currencies—especially those of oil‑importing emerging markets—to swings in oil prices and global dollar liquidity.[2][5]

When oil prices rise, oil‑importing countries must spend more dollars to cover the same volume of crude, which widens their current‑account deficits and weakens their own currencies. For net‑importers reliant on floating exchange‑rate regimes, this shows up as nominal depreciation against the dollar.[2][6] At the same time, oil‑exporting countries accumulate larger stockpiles of dollars, which they typically reinvest into U.S. and other global financial markets, feeding capital flows that can temporarily support dollar strength and compress global yields.[2][5]

From the perspective of a small open economy like the Philippines, this chain means that the country’s external position is constantly tugged by forces outside its control: OPEC‑led production decisions, U.S. monetary policy, and geopolitical shocks in oil‑producing regions all ripple through to the value of the peso.[7][6]

The Philippines as a Vulnerable Oil‑Importer

The Philippines is a classic example of an emerging‑market oil‑importer embedded in the petrodollar system. The country imports more than 90% of its crude oil needs, and its energy matrix remains heavily dependent on imported petroleum products such as diesel, gasoline, and bunker fuel.[7][6] Because the barrel price is set in dollars, every sharp oil‑price spike automatically translates into higher import bills when converted into pesos, even if the peso–dollar rate were to stay unchanged.

In practice, the peso does not stay neutral. Since the early 2020s, the Philippine currency has trended weaker, with the peso depreciating around 10.5% against the dollar in 2022, briefly recovering in 2023, and then losing another 4.2% in 2024.[8] Into early‑mid 2026, the peso has continued to weaken, briefly crossing the 60‑to‑the‑dollar threshold and hovering near or above that level in some months, making it one of the worst‑performing Asian currencies over that period.[7][6] Observers explicitly link this slide to “surging oil prices driven by escalating tensions in the Middle East,” which increases the net‑dollar outflow needed to finance the country’s energy imports.[7][6]

Connecting Petrodollars to Peso Devaluation

The petrodollar system deepens the Philippines’ exposure to the dollar–oil nexus in at least three concrete ways:

  1. Oil‑funded dollar demand: Every time Philippine refiners or state‑backed oil firms must pay for a tanker of crude, dollars are purchased in the foreign‑exchange market. If oil prices spike or payment volumes grow, this extra demand for dollars can push up the dollar–peso rate, all else equal.[7][6]
  2. Current‑account pressure: A higher oil‑import bill worsens the trade balance and, by extension, the current account. Investors and rating agencies read a widening current‑account deficit as a sign of external vulnerability, which can lead to capital‑outflow pressures and downward pressure on the peso.[2][6]
  3. Global dollar strength: The petrodollar system helps keep the dollar structurally strong versus many emerging‑market currencies. When the U.S. Federal Reserve tightens policy or global risk sentiment deteriorates, the dollar often rises as a “safe‑haven” currency, amplifying the peso’s depreciation even if domestic fundamentals alone would not justify such a move.[1][6]

In this context, petrodollar dominance does not directly fix dollarism on the Philippines, but it does make the peso unusually sensitive to the same global shocks that move oil prices and dollar liquidity.[2][4]

From Depreciation to Inflation: The Transmission Channel

Once the peso weakens, the next step is understanding how this affects the cost‑of‑living. The conceptual link is simple: a weaker peso means that each peso buys fewer dollars, and hence fewer dollars‑priced goods, including imported oil, machinery, fertilizers, electronics, and intermediate inputs.[2][8]

However, the measured pass‑through from exchange‑rate movements to consumer‑price inflation in the Philippines is relatively modest. A recent study by the ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO) finds that a 1.0% depreciation of the peso against the U.S. dollar raises quarterly consumer‑price inflation by only about 0.046 percentage point, with a cumulative one‑year effect of roughly 0.091 percentage point.[8] When measured against a broader basket of currencies via the nominal effective exchange rate (NEER), the effect is slightly higher but still limited—around 0.071 percentage point in the short run and 0.147 percentage point over a year.[8]

This “subdued” exchange‑rate pass‑through is attributed to several factors:

  • The credibility of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) in anchoring inflation expectations through a clear inflation‑targeting framework.[8]
  • The structure of domestic pricing, where firms may absorb some exchange‑rate costs rather than immediately passing them fully to consumers.
  • Administrative price controls and subsidies on key commodities, such as fuel and transport, which can temporarily blunt the second‑round effects of peso depreciation.[8]

Nevertheless, petrodollar‑driven shocks often hit via the oil‑price channel first, which can still push inflation higher even if the peso itself depreciates only modestly.[8][6]

Oil Prices, Petrodollars, and Philippine Inflation

Recent data make the interaction clearer. In 2022, Philippine inflation reached 5.8%, followed by 6.0% in 2023, before moderating to 3.2% in 2024—a trajectory that closely tracks movements in international commodity prices, including energy.[8] As oil prices climbed again in 2026 amid Middle East tensions and supply‑side disruptions, headline inflation in the Philippines edged back up, reaching 4.1% in March 2026, with the government and analysts explicitly citing “higher oil and gas prices and a weakening peso” as key drivers.[6][9]

The mechanism is threefold:

  • Higher dollar‑priced crude feeds through into higher pump prices, which directly raises the transport‑and‑fuel component of the consumer‑price index.
  • Energy‑intensive industries (logistics, manufacturing, power generation) face higher input costs, which can be passed on to final goods and services.
  • If the peso also weakens, the dollar‑priced oil becomes even more expensive in local currency terms, amplifying the inflationary impulse.[8][6]

Even though the Bureau of the Treasury reports that the share of imported oil in total Philippine imports is only about 11–15%, its effect on inflation is outsized because it feeds into so many other sectors of the economy.[8][6]

Structural Vulnerabilities Under the Petrodollar Order

For the Philippines, the petrodollar system crystallizes several structural vulnerabilities:

  1. Energy dependence on the dollar–oil nexus: By relying on imported oil priced in dollars, the country’s macroeconomic stability is partially outsourced to the decisions of OPEC, the U.S. Federal Reserve, and Middle East geopolitics.[7][6]
  2. Limited room for autonomous monetary policy: When global oil prices and dollar strength push inflation and external deficits upward, the BSP is often forced to raise interest rates to defend the peso and contain inflation. This can depress domestic demand in an economy that still needs robust growth to reduce poverty and unemployment.[8][6]
  3. Fiscal space depletion: The government may resort to fuel subsidies or selective price controls when oil prices spike, which can widen the fiscal deficit and further erode the peso’s credibility over time.[8][6]

In broader terms, the petrodollar system thus exposes the Philippines to what global economists increasingly describe as carbon‑monetary imperialism: the condition in which emerging‑market economies must finance their energy‑import needs in a foreign currency over which they have no control, while also bearing the macroeconomic costs of global oil‑price volatility.[1][2]

De‑Dollarization and Possible Alternatives

Globally, the petrodollar system is under rising pressure, and the Philippines is not immune to these trends. Some oil‑exporting countries and major trading partners are experimenting with invoicing in alternative currencies (such as the Chinese yuan, euro, or regional baskets) or using non‑dollar payment channels, partly in response to concerns about the “weaponization” of the dollar system through sanctions and financial‑system restrictions.[1][3]

At the same time, the energy transition away from fossil fuels is eroding the material basis of the petrodollar pact. As renewable power, electric vehicles, and energy‑efficient technologies reduce global oil intensity, the underlying demand for dollar‑denominated oil trade may gradually shrink.[1][3] For oil‑importing countries like the Philippines, this could in the longer term reduce the weight of dollar‑priced oil in their external balance sheets, lowering the inflationary and currency‑depreciation impulses generated by oil‑price shocks.[1][6]

Domestically, the Philippines has been promoting energy transition policies—expanded renewables, electric‑vehicle incentives, and public‑transport electrification—but progress remains uneven. Until a more diversified, energy‑secure, and de‑dollarized configuration emerges, the peso will likely remain tethered to the petrodollar‑driven global cycle of oil prices, dollar strength, and inflation.[1][6]

Policy Implications for the Philippines

To reduce the economy’s vulnerability to petrodollar dominance, Philippine policymakers have several levers:

  1. Accelerate the energy transition: Greater investment in solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal capacity, alongside support for grid modernization and storage, can lower the share of imported oil in the energy mix and mute the impact of global oil‑price shocks.[1][6]
  2. Develop regional and non‑dollar payment arrangements: Exploring local‑currency invoicing or clearing arrangements with major trading partners—alongside membership in initiatives such as the ASEAN‑linked payment and settlement systems—could help reduce the peso’s dependence on dollar liquidity for key trade flows.[1][3]
  3. Strengthen external buffers: Maintaining adequate foreign‑exchange reserves, prudent borrowing in both local and foreign currencies, and tight macroprudential rules can help insulate the peso from sudden stops in capital flows and oil‑led current‑account shocks.[2][8]
  4. Enhance monetary‑policy credibility: By continuing to anchor inflation expectations through transparent, data‑driven policy, the BSP can prevent small exchange‑rate movements from snowballing into high and persistent inflation, even when oil‑price shocks and petrodollar‑driven dollar strength occasionally hit the economy.[8][6]

Conclusion: Petrodollars, the Peso, and the Philippine People

In sum, the dominance of petrodollars in global trade does not operate in the abstract; it has tangible consequences for countries like the Philippines that must buy oil in dollars and watch their own currencies fluctuate against an external anchor over which they have no control.[1][2] When oil prices rise or geopolitical tensions push dollar demand upward, the peso often weakens, and this can feed into higher inflation—especially for transportation, utilities, and other energy‑intensive sectors.[8][7][6]

For millions of Filipinos, this means that decisions made in Washington, Riyadh, Moscow, and other centers of oil and finance directly shape the cost of commuting, electricity, and many basic goods. Understanding the petrodollar system is therefore not just an academic exercise; it is a prerequisite for designing policies that can gradually loosen the Philippines’ dependence on imported, dollar‑priced oil and build a more resilient, less dollar‑vulnerable macroeconomic framework for the future.[1][2][6]
