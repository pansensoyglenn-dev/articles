The Feast of Unleavened Bread:
The Discipline of Purging the Infiltration

Glenn Junsay Pansensoy April 3, 2026 5 min read Theology · Feasts · Discipleship

The story of the Cross does not end at the tomb; it flows directly into the next divine appointment. Yeshua’s death was not a historical accident—it was a calculated fulfillment of YHWH’s calendar, occurring precisely at Passover.

As the sun set on the day of His execution, a shift occurred that reverberated through time. The Feast of Unleavened Bread began, signaling a transition from redemption to refining.

The Divine Timing of the Tomb

For seven days, Israel was commanded to remove all leaven from their homes. This wasn't just a lesson in baking; it was a lesson in holiness. Even as the tomb was sealed, Yeshua—our Passover Lamb—lay in the heart of the earth during the very festival dedicated to the removal of corruption.

He was resting in the dust during the season of "putting away what puffs up." It is a seamless progression:

  • Passover: Death and Deliverance.
  • Unleavened Bread: Cleansing and Burial.
  • Firstfruits: Life and Resurrection.

The Nature of Leaven: Small, Silent, and Spreading

In Scripture, leaven (yeast) is rarely a positive symbol. It represents a tiny element that enters a substance and fundamentally alters its nature.

  • The Warning: Yeshua cautioned against the "leaven of the Pharisees"—hypocrisy and corrupted doctrine.
  • The Spread: Paul warned that "a little leaven leavens the whole lump."

Leaven does not conquer by force; it conquers by infiltration. It settles in quietly, working its way through the dough until the original substance is unrecognizable. It creates an illusion of size through inflation.

Seven Days of Intentionality

YHWH commanded a full seven-day cycle for this feast. In the Bible, seven represents completion. By interrupting normal life for a full week, YHWH forces a shift in focus.

  • Heightened Awareness: You cannot eat a single meal without being reminded of the search.
  • From Urgency to Habit: Israel left Egypt in haste—there was no time for bread to rise. But once they were out, that haste had to turn into deliberate intention.

Freedom is first tasted in the "unleavened"—the flat, the simple, and the honest. The ritual of searching every corner of the house for crumbs is a physical exercise meant to train a spiritual reflex: Where has sin gone unnoticed? What have I allowed to stay because it seemed too small to matter?

Sincerity and Truth: The Unleavened Life

When you remove leaven, you aren't left with nothing; you are left with Matzah. Paul calls this the "unleavened bread of sincerity and truth."

Unlike leaven, which inflates and distorts, truth is solid and stable. Unleavened bread is exactly what it appears to be. This is what YHWH is after: a people who are real, not people who appear righteous through "spiritual inflation."

The Pattern of the Messiah

Yeshua is the ultimate "Unleavened One." He was examined and found without fault. He contained no mixture, no hypocrisy, and no internal contradiction. When He was buried during this feast, the sinless "grain" entered the earth without the decay of corruption, perfectly fulfilling the Torah's pattern.

Paul’s instruction to the modern believer is clear: "Let us keep the feast." This isn't about mere nostalgia; it’s about a sequence of spiritual growth:

  • Passover (Redemption) must be followed by Unleavened Bread (Removal).
  • Removal must happen before Shavuot (Filling).
YHWH does not pour His Spirit into a house still cluttered with the leaven of the old life. He clears, then He fills.

Confronting the Mixture

Today, our "leaven" is rarely loud rebellion. It is usually mixture:

  • Scriptural truth blended with human tradition.
  • A "palatable" Messiah reshaped to fit our comfort.
  • Doctrine that prioritizes "what works" over "what YHWH said."

The Feast of Unleavened Bread demands that we stop managing our compromises and start removing them. It calls us back to the Word without addition or subtraction.

The Challenge of the Flat Bread

Living "unleavened" is countercultural because it isn't impressive. It doesn't "rise." It doesn't seek to dominate the room with ego or image. It is simple, honest, and costly.

It is the Word of God stripped of its layers of "almost truth." It is a life that aligns with the Messiah without distortion.

As we move through this season, the question remains: What are we still tolerating? It is time to turn away from the inflated and return to the Unleavened One—moving the ritual from our kitchens into the very fabric of our character.

— Glenn Junsay Pansensoy
